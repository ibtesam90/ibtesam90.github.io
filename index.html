Thesis Title: Reliable test architecture for AI accelerators with test cost reduction

This study aimed to leverage the systolic nature of accelerators and existing data paths to transport the test stimulus to each processing element. Furthermore, the DFT insertion was done at the RTL level rather than at gate-level netlist. We implemented the proposed testing mechanism for different architecture; WS, OS, for low power, and no local reuse. The changes were proposed at the PE level for each architecture.

The results showed the reduced test time in comparison to the traditional scanbased tests and hierarchical based designs. Furthermore, not only the overall switching power was reduced but there was a considerable reduction in peak power. This reduced power consumption ensures the reliability of the accelerator and reduces the yield loss due to IR drop based noise, which may invalidate the test result. Moreover, as the existing data paths were used to transport the test stimulus and their responses, and only additional muxes were introduced the flip flops which were otherwise not controllable or observable, the area overhead was on average less than the scan-based design for testability.

Thesis Title: Dynamic Adjustments of Threshold Temperatures to Get Variable Throughputs in Multi-core Systems

ith the continuous increase in transistor density and increment in processor clock speed, thermal issues, such as thermal shut downs, higher spatial temperature gradients in multi-core processors, hot spots, and reliability degradation has presented industry with a new challenge in further advancement in processor technology. Multi-core-processors have been a solution for some time for these thermal issues but thermal issues have risen again and are presenting researchers with a fresh challenge. Load balancing techniques distributes the load evenly on all the processors to avoid thermal emergencies. This research is a contribution towards tackling the thermal emergencies and reliability of the embedded systems. The proposed technique is load-balancing scheme for low utilization applications/tasks. Thermal issues such as peak temperature and temperature spatial gradients are lowered by varying the throughput of each processor configuration by varying the threshold temperatures. Moreover, processor configurations are selected based on round robin method in order to reduce spatial temperature gradients. The proposed technique also considers the ambient temperature, utilization factor while calculating temperature thresholds for a particular application. The experiments are simulation based and considers independent periodic tasks and task graphs running on homogeneous processors. With the proposed technique, lower peak temperatures, lower temperature spatial gradients, in addition to lower energy consumption, are achieved.

Thesis Title: Home Automation using X-10 Protol

X-10 is communication protocol over the AC voltage. It uses 5ms high frequency bursts to send digital data. Two modules were developed to transmit and receive the data. The transmitter would broadcast a message and all the receivers would capture the incoming data. After capturing the data, the receiver module would then analyze the packet and decide whether the sent packet was intended for it or not. If the data packet was intended for that receiver, the receiver would then continue to execute the sent command. A successful data transmission and receiving was presented. Our group was awarded third position in the open house exhibition for final year projects.
